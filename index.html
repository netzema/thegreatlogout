A public exit from addictive social media

Post why you're leaving. Then leave.

The Great Logout is simple: post for 1 to 7 days, say why you are leaving, tell people where to reach you, then log out. Not silently. Not someday. Visibly. Now.

How it works

One small public exit plan.

You do not need a grand speech. You need a few clear posts, a place people can reach you, and a date when you stop feeding the feed.

Choose 1, 3, or 7 days.

Pick a length you can actually finish. Seven days gives people time to notice.

Post one reason each day.

Write like a person. Say what the feed has cost you, and what you want back.

Tell people where to reach you.

Do not vanish from people you care about. Move the relationship outside the platform.

Delete the apps or deactivate.

Make it harder to drift back in. Remove the shortcut. Break the reflex.

Stay out, and help others do the same.

If someone asks why you left, tell them plainly. That is how the signal spreads.

Why leave

The feed is not just a habit.

This is not only about screen time. A few companies now shape what billions of people see, want, fear, and argue about. They do it for profit, and they do it at scale.

Addictive feeds keep us scrolling long after we meant to stop.

Attention extraction turns spare minutes, outrage, grief, jokes, and friendships into engagement.

Algorithmic pressure decides what rises, what disappears, and what mood the room is in.

Big Tech power is now cultural and political power. Refusing to feed it is a small act, but it is not nothing.

01

Your attention is not raw material.

The platforms need your time, reactions, and relationships. Leaving cuts off the supply.

02

The pressure is real.

Comparison, outrage, and the need to perform leave marks. We should say that without making it soft.

03

Silence is easy to miss.

If you just disappear, the platform absorbs it. A public exit is harder to ignore.

04

You can still be reachable.

The goal is not isolation. The goal is to stop letting platforms sit between you and everyone else.

7-day guide

A week of posts. Then the logout.

The guide gives you one clear prompt per day. Use it as written, change it, or ignore half of it. The point is to finish.

Day 0

Prepare your logout

Save contacts, choose your last day, and decide where people can reach you.

Day 1

Why I am leaving

Start plainly. Say this is intentional.

Day 2

What the feed did to my attention

Name the pull, the drift, the time you want back.

Day 3

What I want back

More quiet. More focus. More actual people. Your reason is yours.

Day 4

Why Big Tech has too much power

Say what you do not want a handful of companies deciding for you.

Day 5

Where to reach me

Give people another path. Keep the connection. Drop the platform.

Day 6

Invite others

No pressure. Just make the door visible.

Day 7

Final post and logout

Post the last message. Delete the apps. Leave.

Manifesto

We are not quitting each other.

We are quitting the systems that turn attention into a business model and call it connection.

We are not leaving because we hate the internet. We are leaving because the platforms changed. They became machines for addiction, outrage, surveillance, and control. The Great Logout is a refusal to keep feeding them with our time, our anger, our friendships, and our attention.

About

Independent, open, accountable.

The Great Logout was initiated by Daniel Netzl, an Austrian data scientist concerned with the social, political, and psychological harms of addictive platform design.

The project is meant to grow beyond one person: supporters, researchers, designers, developers, educators, parents, and people who want out.

We are not affiliated with Instagram, TikTok, Meta, X, or any social media platform.
