Lost in the Scroll

Algorithms do not only waste time. They shape attention, politics, culture, consumption, and the private weather inside our heads. The Great Logout is a public refusal to keep feeding that machine.

By Daniel Netzl February 2025 Updated for The Great Logout

We know how to talk about some addictions. Alcohol, cigarettes, gambling, pills. Their damage is visible enough that society has learned to treat them with caution. We regulate them, warn people about them, and admit that willpower alone is not always enough.

But one of the strongest addictions of our time does not arrive in a bottle or a pack. It sits in a pocket. It glows. It refreshes. It learns. It keeps offering one more clip, one more outrage, one more person to compare yourself to, one more thing to buy, one more reason to stay.

The feed looks harmless because it feels ordinary. That is part of its power.

The feed is designed to win

Platforms like TikTok, Instagram, YouTube, Facebook, and X are not neutral rooms where people simply meet. They are ranking machines. They decide what appears, what vanishes, what gets repeated, and what becomes the mood of the day.

Their business model is simple enough. Keep people watching. Keep people reacting. Keep people returning. More attention means more data. More data means better targeting. Better targeting means more money.

This is why the feed so often feels stronger than your intention. You open it for one thing and leave twenty minutes later with a different mood, a different desire, and no clear memory of choosing any of it.

The point is not that every person inside these companies is evil. The point is colder than that. The system rewards whatever holds attention. If anger holds attention, anger spreads. If envy holds attention, envy spreads. If fear holds attention, fear spreads. If stupidity holds attention, stupidity gets promoted with industrial precision.

A few companies now sit between billions of people and reality. That is too much power.

Big Tech became a private layer over public life

A handful of corporations now shape what billions of people see, believe, want, fear, and argue about. They influence elections, relationships, news, culture, fashion, language, humor, music, outrage, beauty standards, and the sense of what everyone else is supposedly thinking.

This power is not only technical. It is political and cultural. It decides which voices rise and which are buried. It decides what counts as relevant. It decides which conflicts are kept alive because they perform well. It decides what kind of person gets rewarded with attention.

No private platform should have that much influence over the public mind. No company should be able to quietly tune the emotional life of whole societies in order to sell ads.

We would never accept one corporation controlling the front page of every newspaper, the mood of every town square, and the rhythm of every private conversation. Yet we have accepted something close to that because it arrived as entertainment.

Division is profitable

Algorithms do not need to invent every lie or every conflict. They only need to find what keeps people engaged and push it harder. Outrage is useful. Certainty is useful. Tribal contempt is useful. Nuance is usually too slow.

Over time, people stop sharing a common ground. Everyone sees a different world, tuned to their fears, tastes, wounds, and weaknesses. Public debate becomes harder because people are no longer arguing from the same reality.

This is how misinformation travels. This is how radical ideas find people in lonely rooms. This is how conspiracy thinking becomes a social identity. This is how politics becomes performance, and performance becomes rage.

In Austria and elsewhere, we have seen how quickly young people can be pulled into dangerous content streams. The path from boredom to obsession can be short. The path from obsession to harm is not imaginary anymore.

The feed sells dissatisfaction

The same systems that shape opinion also shape desire. They show us bodies, products, holidays, homes, routines, lifestyles, faces, and lives that are arranged to create a feeling of lack.

You are invited to believe that fulfillment is one purchase away. One trip away. One upgrade away. One better version of yourself away. The feed does not need you to be happy. It needs you to be restless.

That restlessness has a cost. It drives consumption, waste, comparison, and debt. It turns identity into a shopping list. It turns travel into proof of existence. It turns private life into content.

The environmental damage is not separate from this. Algorithmic desire moves materials, flights, factories, packaging, emissions, and waste. A trend that lives for two weeks can leave damage that lasts much longer.

Attention is not a small thing

Attention is how we build a life. It is how we listen, learn, love, think, notice, create, grieve, organize, and change. When attention is constantly interrupted, something deeper than productivity is lost.

The feed trains us to expect stimulation before thought has time to form. It makes boredom feel like failure. It makes silence feel suspicious. It makes ordinary life look empty because ordinary life does not refresh every few seconds.

This matters because the world needs more than reaction. It needs patience, imagination, courage, and serious public thought. Climate breakdown, inequality, loneliness, war, democratic decay, and technological power will not be solved by people who have been trained to scroll whenever discomfort appears.

The theft is quiet. It does not always feel like theft. Sometimes it feels like relaxation. Sometimes it feels like staying informed. Sometimes it feels like connection. But if the result is that we are more tired, more cynical, more distracted, and more alone, we should call it what it is.

Screens also keep us still

There is another cost that is harder to measure. A society glued to screens becomes easier to pacify. People can be angry all day and still remain alone in a room. They can watch injustice, react to it, share it, argue about it, and then keep scrolling.

Platforms give us the feeling of participation without asking much from us. A like can feel like a position. A repost can feel like action. A comment can feel like courage. Sometimes these things help. Often they become a substitute for showing up, organizing, talking to people, joining groups, building alternatives, or going into the streets.

This passivity arrives at the worst possible moment. We need active communities, not only informed spectators. We need people who can sit together, disagree, plan, protest, care for each other, and imagine a future that is not handed to them by a feed.

The platforms fragment that common horizon. Each person receives a private version of reality, tuned for engagement and short-term emotion. Shared problems begin to feel too large, too confusing, or too hopeless. Cynicism becomes comfortable. Escape becomes routine.

That is why logging out is not only a personal reset. It is a way to return attention to the places where change can actually happen. Streets. Rooms. Schools. Workplaces. Neighborhoods. Friendships. Public life.

Regulation matters, but refusal matters too

We need real rules for these systems. Platforms should be forced to explain how their algorithms work, what they prioritize, and what harms they create. Personalized feeds should not be the default. Children should not be used as test subjects for engagement machinery. Schools should teach digital literacy as a basic civic skill.

We also need algorithmic impact assessments before massive systems are released into society. If a company can affect elections, mental health, public trust, and the attention of children, it should not be allowed to hide behind the word platform.

But regulation will be slow. The feed is here now. The habit is here now. The damage is here now.

That is where refusal comes in.

Why the logout must be public

Leaving quietly is good for the person who leaves. Leaving publicly is useful for everyone else.

If you simply disappear, the platform absorbs your absence. Nothing changes in the room. People may not even notice. The feed moves on.

But if you post why you are leaving for one day, three days, or seven days, something different happens. People see an exit. They see a person naming the system. They see that logging out is not only a private failure of discipline, but a public decision.

You do not need a perfect argument. You do not need to convince everyone. You only need to make the exit visible.

Say why you are leaving. Tell people where they can reach you. Then leave.

The exit is the message

The Great Logout is not against the internet. It is against platforms that turn addiction, outrage, surveillance, loneliness, and insecurity into a business model.
